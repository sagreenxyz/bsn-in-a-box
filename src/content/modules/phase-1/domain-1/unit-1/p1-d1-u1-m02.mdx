## Learning Objectives

In this module you will develop a working understanding of what it means to practice as a registered nurse within a defined professional scope — not as an abstract rule set, but as a living framework that governs every decision you make at the bedside. By the time you complete this module, you will be able to articulate the purpose and structure of nurse practice acts, explain the relationship between the ANA Nursing Scope and Standards of Practice and state licensure law, distinguish between the registered nurse scope and the scope of unlicensed assistive personnel with concrete clinical examples, describe the principles governing delegation so that you can apply them correctly under pressure, and explain what happens — legally and professionally — when a nurse practices outside their scope.

## What a Scope of Practice Actually Is

The phrase "scope of practice" is one of the most frequently used and least understood in nursing. Students hear it early, often in the context of things they are not allowed to do, and absorb it as a list of prohibitions. That framing misses almost everything important about why scope exists and what it protects.

A scope of practice is a legal and professional boundary drawn around the activities that a particular type of healthcare practitioner is prepared, licensed, and accountable to perform. It is not a ceiling on competence — a skilled nurse may be more clinically capable than many practitioners in adjacent scopes. It is instead a social contract: the public grants nurses the privilege of performing consequential acts upon vulnerable human beings, and in return the profession guarantees that anyone holding the license has met defined preparation standards and accepts defined accountability for their actions.

Understanding this framing transforms how you think about scope. When you decline to perform a task that falls outside your scope, you are not being timid or bureaucratic. You are honoring the contract that makes licensure meaningful. And when you stay within your scope even under pressure to exceed it, you are protecting not only yourself but the patient, who deserves a practitioner who is fully prepared for what they are doing.

## The Architecture of Nursing Law

Nursing practice is regulated at the state level in the United States. Every state has a nurse practice act — a body of law enacted by the state legislature that defines who may practice nursing, under what conditions, and with what accountability. The nurse practice act is the foundational legal document of your professional life. It establishes the existence and authority of the state board of nursing, defines the legal scope of registered nurse practice, specifies the requirements for licensure and license renewal, describes the grounds for disciplinary action, and creates the enforcement mechanisms that give the law its teeth.

The state board of nursing is the administrative body that implements the nurse practice act. The board writes the administrative rules that flesh out the statute's requirements, processes licensure applications, investigates complaints, and conducts disciplinary hearings. When a nurse's license is at risk, it is the board of nursing that acts — not a hospital, not a professional association, not a court (unless criminal conduct is alleged). This is why your relationship with your state board of nursing is one of the most important professional relationships you will ever have.

Nurse practice acts vary meaningfully from state to state, which creates a challenge in an era of interstate practice. The Nurse Licensure Compact (NLC) addresses this by creating a multistate license that allows nurses to practice in all compact member states under a single license governed by the rules of the nurse's primary state of residence. As of 2024, the majority of states have joined the NLC, but the details matter — if you are considering practicing in a state that is not your primary residence, or considering travel nursing, you need to verify the current NLC membership status of that state.

## The ANA Scope and Standards: The Profession's Self-Definition

While the nurse practice act is law — binding regardless of what the profession says — the American Nurses Association Nursing Scope and Standards of Practice is the profession's own authoritative statement of what registered nurses do and how they should do it. The current edition, published in 2021, defines nursing practice and establishes sixteen standards: six standards of practice covering the nursing process and ten standards of professional performance covering the conduct, knowledge, and accountability expected of every registered nurse.

The standards of practice follow the nursing process: assessment, diagnosis, outcomes identification, planning, implementation, and evaluation. Each standard specifies what the registered nurse must do, using the language of competency rather than aspiration. The registered nurse collects comprehensive data pertinent to the healthcare consumer's health and situation. The registered nurse analyzes the assessment data to determine actual or potential diagnoses, problems, and issues. These are not suggestions — they are the professional baseline that defines acceptable practice.

The standards of professional performance address dimensions of practice that transcend any individual patient encounter: ethics, advocacy, respectful and equitable practice, communication, collaboration, leadership, education, evidence-based practice and research, quality of practice, professional practice evaluation, resource utilization, and environmental health. These standards are where nursing's broader professional responsibilities live — your obligation not only to individual patients but to the health of communities and the advancement of the profession.

## Delegation: The Highest-Stakes Application of Scope

One of the most practically important and most frequently misunderstood aspects of scope of practice is delegation — the transfer of responsibility for performing a nursing task to another person while retaining accountability for the outcome. Delegation is not assignment. When a nurse assigns a task to another registered nurse, the assigning nurse transfers both the task and the professional accountability for it. Delegation to unlicensed assistive personnel (UAP) is different in kind: the nurse transfers the task but retains full accountability for whether the task was appropriate to delegate, whether the right person was selected, whether adequate direction and supervision were provided, and whether the outcome was acceptable.

The National Council of State Boards of Nursing has articulated the Five Rights of Delegation as a framework for making delegation decisions correctly under clinical pressure. The right task is one that is delegatable under the state practice act and agency policy. The right circumstance requires that the patient's condition and care setting make delegation safe. The right person requires that the UAP has the training, demonstrated competency, and agency authorization to perform the task. The right direction and communication requires that the nurse gives clear, specific instructions including expected results and parameters for reporting back. The right supervision and evaluation requires that the nurse monitors the outcome and is available to intervene.

A clinical illustration clarifies why these rights matter. Suppose you are caring for Eleanor Vasquez on a busy medical-surgical floor. You need her vital signs taken and her blood glucose checked. You can delegate both of these tasks to a certified nursing assistant — they fall within the CNA scope, Eleanor's condition is stable enough that the data is being collected for monitoring rather than crisis response, and the CNA on your unit is competent and authorized to perform glucometry. What you cannot delegate is the interpretation of those values and the decision about what to do with them. When Eleanor's blood pressure comes back at 168/94 and her glucose at 287, the CNA reports the numbers. The clinical judgment — is this a change from her baseline, what does the combination of these values mean for her kidneys, does the physician need to be called — is yours and yours alone.

## When Scope and Ethics Appear to Conflict

The relationship between a nurse's legal scope and their ethical obligations is generally harmonious — both direct the nurse toward safe, competent, evidence-based care. But there are situations in which they appear to conflict, and understanding how to navigate those moments is a core professional competency.

Consider a situation in which a physician gives a verbal order that the nurse believes is incorrect or potentially harmful. The nurse's legal scope does not include refusing all physician orders — nursing practice occurs within an interprofessional system, and the nurse is not the primary prescriber. But the nurse's ethical obligations are clear: the nurse has a duty to the patient that is independent of any hierarchical relationship, and that duty requires speaking up when patient safety is at risk. The ANA Code of Ethics, Provision 3, states that the nurse promotes, advocates for, and protects the rights, health, and safety of the patient. This means that a nurse who silently carries out a harmful order is not complying with the law — they are violating the ethical and professional standards that are equally part of their scope.

The practical resolution is structured advocacy: the nurse communicates the concern clearly and professionally, documents the concern and the response, escalates through the chain of command if the initial response is inadequate, and ultimately refuses to carry out an order they believe will cause harm — accepting that refusal may require documentation, incident reporting, and further professional dialogue. Nurses who understand their scope understand that this kind of advocacy is not insubordination. It is professionalism.

## Concept Synthesis

This module contributes directly to your understanding of the concepts of **ethics**, **safety**, **communication**, **leadership**, and **collaboration** as they appear across the BSN curriculum.

Ethics in nursing is not a set of abstract principles that apply only in dramatic end-of-life situations — it is the daily discipline of practicing in a way that honors your obligations to patients, colleagues, the profession, and society. The nurse practice act and the ANA standards are both expressions of nursing ethics institutionalized as law and professional expectation. Understanding scope is understanding ethics made concrete.

Safety is the foundational argument for scope of practice. The boundaries exist because scope violations — practitioners performing tasks they are not prepared for — cause preventable harm. Delegation errors are among the most common sources of preventable adverse events in acute care settings. Every element of this module connects to safety as its ultimate justification.

Communication is woven through scope issues at every level — the nurse-physician communication that is required when an order is unsafe, the nurse-UAP communication that makes delegation effective, the nurse-patient communication that requires interpreter services for limited-English-proficiency patients. Scope governs who communicates what to whom, and understanding scope is prerequisite to communicating effectively within the healthcare team.

Leadership and collaboration are inseparable from scope in practice. You lead when you model correct delegation, when you advocate for patients within the interprofessional team, and when you refuse to look away from unsafe practice. You collaborate when you understand what each team member's scope enables and constrains, and when you build care plans that deploy the right person for each task.
