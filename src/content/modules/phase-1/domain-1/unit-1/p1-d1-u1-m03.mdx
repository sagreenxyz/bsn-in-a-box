## Learning Objectives

This module builds ethical reasoning capacity rather than delivering a list of rules to memorize. By the time you complete this module, you will be able to analyze clinical ethical conflicts using the four bioethical principles — autonomy, beneficence, nonmaleficence, and justice — not as isolated definitions but as analytical lenses that reveal different dimensions of the same situation. You will be able to describe each of the nine provisions of the ANA Code of Ethics in your own words and explain how each provision shapes nursing behavior in practice. You will apply at least one structured ethical decision-making model to a clinical scenario, articulate the difference between a nurse's personal moral beliefs and their professional ethical obligations, and recognize the specific ethical challenges most commonly encountered in nursing practice — patient autonomy conflicts, resource allocation decisions, end-of-life care, and professional boundary issues.

## Why Ethical Reasoning Cannot Be Reduced to Rules

A student new to bioethics often hopes that ethics will function like pharmacology: learn the principle, apply it, get the answer. This hope is understandable but mistaken, and understanding why it is mistaken is itself the first step toward genuine ethical competence.

Ethical principles conflict. A patient with terminal cancer has the right to refuse further chemotherapy — autonomy supports this. The nurse who cares for this patient also has a beneficence obligation to promote the patient's wellbeing, and believes, based on prognostic data, that the patient would have meaningful quality time with continued treatment. The patient's adult children, who will be devastated by the loss, appeal to the team to convince their parent to continue treatment. Justice, in the form of fair resource use, enters when the extended hospital stay required for that continued treatment occupies resources that other patients also need. No single principle resolves this situation. The nurse who says "autonomy wins, end of discussion" has not engaged in ethical reasoning — they have avoided it by applying a hierarchy they have not examined.

Ethical reasoning is the structured practice of holding multiple principles in simultaneous view, identifying where they converge and where they conflict, understanding whose interests are at stake and how they weigh against each other, and arriving at a position you can articulate and defend — knowing that reasonable people with equally rigorous ethical thinking may arrive at a different position. This is not relativism. Some ethical conclusions are more defensible than others, and rigorous reasoning produces better conclusions than unreflective intuition. But the rigor is in the process, not in the memorization of a ranked list of principles.

<ThresholdConcept title="Autonomy Is Not Absolute — and Neither Is Beneficence">
The principle of autonomy — that competent adults have the right to make their own healthcare decisions — is central to contemporary Western bioethics and is deeply embedded in nursing's professional values. But understanding autonomy as a threshold concept means grasping something that trips up even experienced clinicians: autonomy is a right of the person, not a trump card that ends every ethical discussion.

Autonomy applies when the person is competent to exercise it. Competence is not the same as agreement with what the clinician recommends — a patient who refuses insulin for religious reasons may be fully competent. But a patient whose decision-making is substantially impaired by delirium, active psychosis, acute intoxication, or dementia may not be exercising genuine autonomous choice. The nurse's ethical obligation in these situations is not to simply carry out whatever the patient says, but to assess the quality of the decision-making process — is the patient receiving adequate information in a form they can understand, are they free from coercion, do their current stated preferences reflect their considered values?

Crossing this threshold — understanding that respecting autonomy sometimes requires more active engagement than simply complying with a stated preference — permanently changes how you understand patient-centered care. You move from a model in which "respecting the patient" means doing whatever they ask to a model in which "respecting the patient" means ensuring their decisions reflect who they actually are when they are fully themselves.
</ThresholdConcept>

## The Four Principles: Analytical Lenses, Not a Checklist

Tom Beauchamp and James Childress introduced the four-principle framework for biomedical ethics in 1979, and it remains the dominant framework in clinical ethics because it captures the moral dimensions that most consistently appear in healthcare conflicts. The four principles — autonomy, beneficence, nonmaleficence, and justice — do not themselves resolve ethical conflicts, but they give clinicians a structured vocabulary for identifying what is at stake.

**Autonomy** derives from the Greek words for "self" and "law" — it refers to the capacity of a person to govern themselves, to make decisions according to their own values and reasoning rather than being governed by external authority. In healthcare, the principle of autonomy grounds the requirements for informed consent, the right to refuse treatment, the obligation to maintain confidentiality, and the duty to provide complete and accurate information. A nurse who withholds a terminal diagnosis from a patient because they think the patient "can't handle it" — even with genuine benevolent intent — is violating the principle of autonomy by substituting their own judgment for the patient's right to self-determination.

**Beneficence** is the obligation to act in ways that benefit patients. It is the positive duty to promote good, not merely to avoid harm. Beneficence is what drives nurses to go beyond the minimum required tasks to actively assess, educate, advocate, and coordinate care. It is also the source of paternalism when it runs unchecked — the impulse to impose one's judgment of what is good for the patient over the patient's own understanding of what is good for themselves. Recognizing when beneficent intent is sliding into paternalism is a clinical skill that requires ongoing self-examination.

**Nonmaleficence** — the obligation to avoid causing harm — is the principle most people encounter first, usually via the Latin phrase *primum non nocere*, "first do no harm." In practice, nonmaleficence is rarely the simple prohibition it appears to be, because virtually every intervention carries some risk of harm. The application of nonmaleficence is not "never do anything that might cause harm" but rather "ensure that the expected benefits are proportionate to the foreseeable risks, and that harm is not inflicted without justification." The nurse who refuses to administer a necessary but painful injection because they want to spare the patient discomfort is not applying nonmaleficence — they are misapplying it in a way that causes greater harm through omission.

**Justice** in bioethics concerns the fair distribution of benefits, risks, and burdens. It includes procedural justice (are the processes for making decisions fair and consistent?) and distributive justice (are healthcare resources allocated fairly across the population?). For nurses at the bedside, justice most often appears in questions about equitable care — does this patient receive the same quality of assessment, communication, and advocacy regardless of their race, insurance status, language, or diagnosis? The evidence that implicit bias affects clinical care, including nursing care, makes justice not an abstract principle but a daily practice requiring self-awareness and corrective action.

Jordan's expression shifts — they look uncomfortable but say nothing immediately. After the physician leaves, Jordan says quietly to you, "I don't think I want to take a benzo. Consider what the four principles reveal about this situation. Autonomy: Jordan has expressed a clear preference and articulated specific reasoning for it. That preference deserves to be heard and taken seriously. Beneficence: both the physician's recommendation and Jordan's preference are motivated by concern for Jordan's wellbeing — they just reflect different assessments of what "wellbeing" means in this context. Nonmaleficence: both options carry risks — untreated sleep disturbance has real consequences, and benzodiazepine dependence is a well-documented risk, particularly in patients with anxiety disorders. Justice: Jordan's hesitation to assert their preference because they don't want to seem "difficult" may reflect prior experiences of having their concerns dismissed, a dynamic that disproportionately affects marginalized patients including nonbinary individuals. Your role as the nurse is to create the conditions in which Jordan can exercise their autonomy — which means actively inviting Jordan to share their concerns with the physician and supporting that conversation, not simply documenting that the physician's recommendation was communicated.

## The ANA Code of Ethics: A Living Professional Document

The ANA Code of Ethics for Nurses with Interpretive Statements, last revised in 2015, is the nursing profession's authoritative ethical standard. Unlike a legal statute, the Code does not enumerate prohibited behaviors — it articulates the values, obligations, and ideals that define what it means to practice as a registered nurse. All nine provisions reward careful reading rather than memorization.

Provision 1 establishes that the nurse practices with compassion and respect for the inherent dignity, worth, and unique attributes of every person. The practical implication is that dignity is not conditional on the patient's behavior, diagnosis, or social status. A nurse who provides less attentive care to a patient who is verbally abusive, or who fails to advocate for a patient whose addiction is seen as self-inflicted, is violating Provision 1 — even if their care is technically within standards.

Provision 2 states that the nurse's primary commitment is to the patient — whether an individual, family, group, community, or population. This provision is the basis for the nurse's advocacy role and for the nurse's obligation to speak up when institutional pressures, cost concerns, or interprofessional dynamics are working against the patient's interests.

Provision 3 is the advocacy provision: the nurse promotes, advocates for, and protects the rights, health, and safety of the patient. This is the provision that obligates nurses to report unsafe practice, to use chain-of-command processes when a care decision seems unsafe, and to refuse to participate in care they believe will harm the patient.

Provision 4 addresses accountability: the nurse has authority, accountability, and responsibility for nursing practice, and is responsible for making judgments and taking actions consistent with the obligation to promote health and to provide optimal care. This provision is the professional basis for why nurses cannot simply defer to "but the doctor ordered it" when an order is unsafe — the nurse's accountability is their own, not derivative.

Provisions 5 through 9 extend nursing's ethical obligations beyond the individual patient encounter. Provision 5 addresses the nurse's duties to themselves — including the obligation to maintain their own health and integrity, and to continue growing professionally. Provision 6 addresses the nurse's responsibility for improving the care environment. Provision 7 addresses the nurse's obligation to advance the profession through knowledge development and dissemination. Provision 8 addresses the nurse's responsibility to promote health for all people. Provision 9 articulates the nursing profession's collective responsibility to articulate and sustain its values in society.

## Ethical Decision-Making in Practice

When a nurse encounters an ethical conflict — and they will, often — they need more than principles. They need a process. Several ethical decision-making models exist; all share a common logic: identify the relevant facts, identify the stakeholders and their interests, apply ethical principles, consider available options, decide, act, and evaluate.

The most clinically useful model for nurses follows these steps. First, gather the facts: what is actually happening, what do the relevant parties actually want, and what information might be incomplete or inaccurate? Second, identify the ethical issue: is this a conflict between principles, a rights violation, a justice concern, a question of appropriate decision-making authority? Third, identify the stakeholders: who is affected and what are their interests? Fourth, consider options: what are the possible courses of action, and what are the ethical implications of each? Fifth, make a decision you can defend: choose the option that best honors the ethical obligations at stake and that you could explain to a reasonable colleague. Sixth, implement and evaluate: take action, document your reasoning, and assess whether the outcome aligns with the ethical goal.

A critical practical note: ethical decision-making in nursing rarely happens in isolation or without institutional support. Ethics consultations, ethics committees, patient advocates, chaplains, and social workers are all resources. Using them is not weakness — it is professional practice. The most experienced clinicians know that complex ethical situations benefit from multiple perspectives.

## Moral Distress: The Ethical Experience of Nursing

Ethical reasoning is not only a cognitive exercise — it is an emotional and professional one. Moral distress is the experience of knowing the ethically right course of action but being constrained by institutional, legal, or interprofessional factors from taking it. It is one of the most significant contributors to nursing burnout and is directly correlated with intent to leave the profession.

Moral distress is not the same as being in an ethically complex situation. It specifically arises when the nurse perceives that they know what should be done but cannot do it — when a patient is receiving care the nurse believes is futile but the family insists on continuing, when a nurse witnesses a colleague providing unsafe care but the reporting system seems ineffective, when staffing levels fall below what safe care requires and the nurse cannot make up the difference.

Recognizing moral distress — in yourself and in colleagues — is the first step toward addressing it. Moral resilience, the capacity to sustain ethical practice in the face of adversity, is a professional competency that can be developed. It involves having a clear sense of your own values, maintaining connections with colleagues who share those values, using institutional resources like ethics committees and employee assistance programs, and engaging in reflection that transforms difficult experiences into professional growth rather than unprocessed trauma.

## Concept Synthesis

This module contributes to your understanding of **ethics**, **communication**, **coping**, **stress**, and **care coordination** as they thread across all four phases of this curriculum.

Ethics is not a discrete topic to be mastered in one module — it is the lens through which nursing practice is continuously examined. Every module in this curriculum touches ethics somewhere: the ethics of informed consent in medication administration, the ethics of assessment documentation, the ethics of clinical resource allocation. This module provides the conceptual vocabulary and reasoning process for those ongoing examinations.

Communication is the medium through which ethical obligations are enacted. The nurse who believes a patient's autonomy is being violated must communicate that belief — to the patient, to the team, to supervisors if necessary. Ethical intent without effective communication produces no ethical outcome.

Coping and stress are directly implicated by moral distress — the most significant ethical challenge specific to the nursing profession. Understanding the ethical dimensions of your work, developing a vocabulary for naming what you are experiencing, and knowing where to seek support are the foundations of a sustainable ethical practice.

Care coordination as an ethical obligation appears in Provisions 2 and 3 of the Code of Ethics: the nurse's primary commitment to the patient includes ensuring that care across the continuum is coordinated, and that patients are not lost between settings or disciplines.
