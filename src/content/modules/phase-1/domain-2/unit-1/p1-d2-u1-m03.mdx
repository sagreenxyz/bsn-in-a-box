## Learning Objectives

Pain is the most common reason people seek healthcare and the symptom that nurses most frequently assess and respond to — and it is also the assessment that is most frequently done inadequately. By completing this module, you will be able to conduct a comprehensive pain assessment using validated assessment tools appropriate for a range of patient populations including those who cannot self-report, explain the physiological mechanisms of nociceptive and neuropathic pain and how those mechanisms inform the selection of analgesic approaches, describe the barriers to accurate pain assessment including patient reluctance to report, provider bias, and assessment tool limitations, identify the nursing interventions — pharmacological and non-pharmacological — that constitute a comprehensive pain management approach, and recognize special populations for whom standard pain assessment tools are inappropriate and apply validated alternatives.

## Pain as a Subjective Experience

The foundational principle of pain assessment — established by the American Pain Society in 1996 when it formally designated pain as the "fifth vital sign" — is that pain is whatever the experiencing person says it is, occurring whenever they say it occurs. This principle is not merely a compassionate aspiration; it reflects the biological reality that pain is a subjective experience generated in the brain in response to neural signals, and that the same nociceptive input produces vastly different pain experiences in different people and in the same person at different times.

The clinical implication of pain's subjectivity is that the nurse's assessment of a patient's pain must center the patient's self-report. Observational cues — grimacing, guarding, elevated heart rate and blood pressure — are supplementary, not primary. A patient who rates their pain as 8/10 and appears calm is having 8/10 pain. A patient who rates their pain as 2/10 and appears distressed may be experiencing psychological distress that is distinct from pain, or may be minimizing their pain for cultural or situational reasons. The assessment does not end with the number — it extends to the clinical story that the number is part of.

## Comprehensive Pain Assessment: Beyond the Number

A numerical rating scale — "rate your pain from zero to ten" — is a starting point, not a complete pain assessment. The comprehensive pain assessment addresses the full character of the pain experience using frameworks like OLDCART (Onset, Location, Duration, Characteristics, Aggravating factors, Relieving factors, Treatment) or the PQRSTU mnemonic. Each element provides clinically meaningful information.

Onset and duration distinguish acute pain, which follows a predictable trajectory related to tissue injury or illness, from chronic pain, which persists beyond expected healing time and involves different neural mechanisms and different management approaches. A patient who reports that they have had this pain "for years" and that it is "the same as always" is describing a chronic pain condition that requires a fundamentally different assessment and management approach than a patient reporting pain that began acutely four hours ago.

Location and radiation reveal which structures are involved. Pain that is well-localized to a specific body area is more likely to reflect somatic structures — bones, muscles, skin — while visceral pain is often poorly localized, deep, and described as pressure, squeezing, or cramping. Referred pain — pain perceived at a location distant from its source, as in left shoulder pain from diaphragmatic irritation or jaw pain from myocardial ischemia — requires knowledge of dermatome and referred pain patterns to interpret correctly.

Characteristics describe the quality of the pain: sharp, burning, aching, throbbing, stabbing, pressure, crushing. These descriptors distinguish nociceptive pain (sharp, throbbing, aching) from neuropathic pain (burning, electric, shooting, pins-and-needles), which has important implications for pharmacological management — nociceptive pain responds to standard analgesics, while neuropathic pain often requires adjuvant medications like anticonvulsants or tricyclic antidepressants.

Ruth Abramowitz returned to the hospital three days after her hip fracture surgery. She is now in a step-down unit, and her daughter Sarah reports that Ruth "isn't acting like herself" — more confused than baseline, restless, and picking at her IV line. Ruth's dementia makes the numerical rating scale unreliable: when asked to rate her pain from zero to ten, she sometimes says "twelve" and sometimes looks blankly at you and asks what you mean.

This is exactly the clinical situation where standard pain assessment tools fail and validated alternatives are required. The PAINAD scale (Pain Assessment in Advanced Dementia) was developed specifically for patients who cannot reliably self-report, using five behavioral indicators: breathing pattern, negative vocalization, facial expression, body language, and consolability. Each is rated zero to two, for a maximum score of ten. A score of one to three indicates mild pain, four to six indicates moderate pain, and seven to ten indicates severe pain.

Observing Ruth carefully, you note that her breathing is normal, but she is moaning softly even when not touched, her facial expression shows a furrowed brow and tightened jaw, she is restless and shifting position frequently, and she is difficult to console — her agitation returns quickly when her daughter stops speaking to her. Her PAINAD score is 6, indicating moderate pain. The nursing response is not to wait for Sarah to confirm that Ruth "seems uncomfortable" — it is to act on the assessment, communicate to the physician that Ruth appears to be in moderate pain based on behavioral assessment, and initiate multimodal pain management that includes scheduled rather than PRN analgesics (because Ruth is unlikely to consistently request pain medication) alongside non-pharmacological approaches including repositioning, heat, and presence.

## Barriers to Accurate Pain Assessment

Despite the fifth vital sign designation and decades of nursing education about pain assessment, undertreated pain remains pervasive in healthcare — and its distribution is not random. The barriers to accurate pain assessment fall into three categories: patient-level barriers, provider-level barriers, and systemic barriers.

Patient-level barriers include cultural and personal beliefs that stoicism is expected or admirable, fear that reporting pain means the disease is worsening, fear of addiction from opioid analgesics, reluctance to complain or be seen as demanding, and the linguistic and cognitive barriers that prevent reliable self-report in some populations.

Provider-level barriers include implicit bias — the well-documented finding that Black patients, women, older adults, and patients with substance use histories consistently receive less adequate pain assessment and treatment than white patients, men, younger adults, and patients without substance use histories for the same conditions. They also include undertreated pain skepticism, time pressure that reduces the thoroughness of assessment, and insufficient knowledge about effective multimodal pain management.

Systemic barriers include fragmented care that disrupts pain management continuity, formulary restrictions that limit access to preferred analgesics, inadequate staffing that reduces assessment frequency, and regulatory environments that create barriers to appropriate opioid prescribing in response to the opioid crisis — sometimes at the cost of undertreating patients with legitimate pain needs.

## Non-Pharmacological Pain Management

The pharmacological management of pain — analgesics, adjuvants, and procedural interventions — occurs within a nursing scope that also includes a substantial array of non-pharmacological approaches that the nurse can implement independently. These approaches are not replacements for adequate pharmacological management when it is indicated; they are complements that improve pain control, reduce analgesic requirements, and respect the patient's preference for treatment modalities.

Positioning and immobilization reduce pain from musculoskeletal and postoperative sources by removing mechanical stress from injured structures. Heat and cold application produce pain relief through distinct mechanisms: heat increases local blood flow and muscle relaxation, reducing aching and spasm; cold reduces inflammation and numbs local pain pathways, most effective in the first 48 hours following acute injury. Distraction — including music, conversation, guided imagery, and virtual reality — activates descending pain modulation pathways and is particularly effective in procedural pain.

Cognitive-behavioral approaches including relaxation training, mindfulness-based stress reduction, and biofeedback have substantial evidence for effectiveness in chronic pain management and are increasingly supported for acute pain as well. The nurse's role in these approaches is to introduce patients to techniques, provide basic instruction and practice, and refer to specialists — psychologists, pain management nurses, or integrative medicine practitioners — for more intensive intervention.
