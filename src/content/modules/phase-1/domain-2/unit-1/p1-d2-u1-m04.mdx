## Learning Objectives

The systematic head-to-toe physical examination is the organized, reproducible clinical assessment that ensures nothing is missed — because it forces the nurse to move through every body system in a defined sequence rather than assessing only what the patient complains about or what seems most relevant to the primary problem. By completing this module, you will be able to perform a systematic head-to-toe physical examination in the correct sequence, explain the normal findings expected at each body region and system, recognize common abnormal findings and their clinical significance, distinguish the assessment sequence used for acute monitoring from the more comprehensive focused and complete examination approaches, and adapt the examination approach appropriately for the clinical context including postoperative monitoring, emergency assessment, and routine health maintenance.

## The Logic of Systematic Assessment

A systematic approach to physical examination exists for the same reason a pilot uses a checklist before takeoff: because the human mind, under conditions of clinical complexity and time pressure, reliably misses things it is not systematically prompted to look for. The head-to-toe sequence — beginning at the skull and moving downward through the neurological, sensory, cardiovascular, respiratory, gastrointestinal, genitourinary, musculoskeletal, and integumentary systems — creates a reproducible framework that the nurse can execute even when distracted, tired, or managing multiple simultaneous demands.

The complete physical examination taught in health assessment courses is not what nurses perform on every patient at every assessment in clinical practice. A postoperative patient assessment focuses on the surgical site, cardiovascular and respiratory status, level of consciousness, and pain — a focused assessment of the systems most at risk in the early postoperative period. An emergency assessment may begin with the ABCDE framework — Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability (neurological status), Exposure (skin) — as a rapid triage tool before proceeding to a more detailed assessment. The shift assessment performed by a medical-surgical nurse every four to eight hours is a systematic focused assessment that covers all systems but emphasizes changes from prior assessment. The complete, detailed head-to-toe examination with all auscultation, percussion, and palpation is typically performed on admission and at other defined intervals.

Understanding which assessment is appropriate for each clinical situation — and being able to perform each competently — is the practical goal of this module.

## Head and Neurological Assessment

The neurological assessment begins with the patient's level of consciousness and orientation, the most sensitive early indicators of brain function change. The Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) provides a standardized numerical score for level of consciousness based on eye opening, verbal response, and motor response, with a maximum score of 15 indicating full consciousness and a minimum score of 3 indicating deep unconsciousness. Orientation is assessed by asking the patient to identify their name, the location (hospital, city), and the date or at minimum the year — orientation to person is the last to be lost and the first to return, while orientation to time is the most sensitive to disruption.

Pupillary assessment — size, equality, and reactivity to light — provides direct information about the third cranial nerve and, by extension, brainstem function and intracranial pressure. Normal pupils are 2-6mm in diameter, equal bilaterally, and briskly reactive to light. Unequal pupils (anisocoria) can be a normal variant (present in approximately 20% of the population) or a sign of serious pathology including herniation. A unilateral fixed and dilated pupil in a patient who has had a change in mental status is a neurological emergency.

Cranial nerve assessment can be performed rapidly and provides important diagnostic information. Facial symmetry, extraocular movements, gag reflex, tongue deviation, and grip strength are among the components that can be assessed in a focused neurological check and that identify lateralizing deficits consistent with stroke or mass lesion.

## Cardiovascular and Respiratory Assessment

Cardiovascular physical examination includes auscultation of heart sounds at the four primary auscultatory areas — the aortic area (right second intercostal space), the pulmonic area (left second intercostal space), Erb's point (left third intercostal space), and the tricuspid and mitral areas (left fourth and fifth intercostal spaces, the latter at the midclavicular line). Normal heart sounds are S1 (the "lub" produced by mitral and tricuspid valve closure at the beginning of systole) and S2 (the "dub" produced by aortic and pulmonic valve closure at the beginning of diastole). Additional sounds — S3, S4, murmurs, rubs — indicate pathology that requires further evaluation.

Respiratory assessment through auscultation identifies breath sounds that indicate the movement of air through the airways. Normal breath sounds vary by location: vesicular sounds are soft and low-pitched over most of the lung fields; bronchial sounds, louder and higher-pitched, are normal over the trachea but abnormal over peripheral lung tissue; bronchovesicular sounds are normal over the main-stem bronchi. Adventitious sounds — crackles, rhonchi, wheezes, and pleural friction rubs — are abnormal and indicate specific pathological processes that the nurse must recognize.

## Integumentary and Musculoskeletal Assessment

The skin is often the most informative organ in a head-to-toe assessment — it reflects systemic status, vascular perfusion, fluid balance, nutritional state, and medication effects, and is the site of pressure injury, the most common and most preventable hospital-acquired condition. Skin assessment includes inspection for color (pallor, cyanosis, jaundice, erythema), texture, moisture, turgor (assessed by gently pinching and observing how quickly the skin returns to its original position), and integrity (noting any wounds, rashes, lesions, or pressure injury stages).

Pressure injury prevention begins with assessment — specifically, using a validated risk assessment tool such as the Braden Scale on admission and at defined intervals thereafter. The Braden Scale assesses six subscales: sensory perception, moisture, activity, mobility, nutrition, and friction and shear. A total score of 18 or below indicates at-risk status requiring preventive interventions including repositioning every two hours, pressure-redistribution surfaces, moisture management, nutritional optimization, and skin care.

Musculoskeletal assessment addresses range of motion, muscle strength, joint integrity, and gait. It is particularly important in patients at fall risk — the combination of impaired mobility, altered gait, cognitive impairment, and polypharmacy that creates fall risk requires a comprehensive musculoskeletal and neurological assessment to quantify. The Morse Fall Scale or similar validated tool provides a standardized risk assessment that drives the nursing fall prevention plan.
