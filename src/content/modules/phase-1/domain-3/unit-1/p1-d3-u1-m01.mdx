## Learning Objectives

Medication administration is simultaneously the most common nursing procedure and one of the highest-risk nursing activities — and those two facts are deeply related. By completing this module, you will be able to apply the rights of medication administration as a systematic safety framework, explain the pharmacokinetic processes of absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion and how patient-specific factors alter each, describe the nursing responsibilities before, during, and after medication administration, identify high-alert medications and explain why they require additional safety precautions, explain the routes of medication administration and the nursing considerations specific to each, recognize common adverse drug reactions and explain the nursing response, and describe the nurse's role in medication error prevention, disclosure, and reporting.

<ThresholdConcept title="Medication Administration Is Not Execution — It Is Clinical Judgment">
The most dangerous belief a nursing student can hold about medication administration is that the nurse's job is to carry out the medication order — to execute the physician's prescription as written, verify the rights as a procedural checklist, and document the administration. This belief is dangerous because it frames the nurse as a technician executing a prescription rather than a clinician applying pharmacological knowledge to a specific patient in a specific clinical context.

The nurse who approaches medication administration as clinical judgment asks: Is this medication still appropriate given this patient's current status? Is the dose correct for this patient's weight, renal function, and age? Has anything changed since this order was written that affects the appropriateness of giving this medication right now? Does the patient understand what this medication is for and what to expect? Are the vital signs within parameters that make it safe to administer?

These questions are not administrative hurdles — they are the clinical reasoning that prevents medication errors. The five rights of medication administration — right patient, right drug, right dose, right route, right time — are a minimum safety check, not a complete safety process. The nurse's clinical judgment is the final line of defense in the medication administration system, and understanding that responsibility is the threshold concept that transforms medication administration from a routine task into a high-stakes clinical act.
</ThresholdConcept>

## Pharmacokinetics: How Drugs Move Through the Body

Pharmacokinetics describes what the body does to a drug — how it is absorbed, distributed to its sites of action, metabolized into active or inactive forms, and excreted. Every one of these processes can be altered by patient-specific factors including age, organ function, body composition, and concurrent medications, which is why the same dose of the same drug produces different effects in different patients.

**Absorption** is the movement of drug from the site of administration into the systemic circulation. The route of administration is the primary determinant of absorption rate and completeness. Intravenous administration bypasses absorption entirely — the drug is delivered directly to the bloodstream at 100% bioavailability. Oral administration requires absorption through the gastrointestinal mucosa, a process affected by gastric pH, GI motility, food content, and the drug's own chemical properties. Subcutaneous and intramuscular routes provide slower, more sustained absorption than IV but faster onset than oral for many medications.

**Distribution** is the movement of drug from the bloodstream to tissues. Distribution depends on the drug's ability to cross tissue membranes (determined by lipid solubility and molecular size), its protein binding (drugs bound to plasma proteins like albumin are pharmacologically inactive until released), and the volume of distribution — a theoretical measure of how widely a drug is dispersed in the body. Drugs with high lipid solubility distribute widely into fat compartments — an important consideration for obese patients, where fat-soluble drugs accumulate and have prolonged effects.

**Metabolism** is the chemical transformation of drugs, primarily in the liver, from pharmacologically active compounds to forms that can be excreted. The cytochrome P450 enzyme system performs most hepatic drug metabolism and is the mechanism for the majority of drug-drug interactions — when one drug inhibits or induces a CYP450 enzyme, it alters the metabolism of other drugs processed by the same enzyme, leading to unexpectedly high or low drug levels. Renal and hepatic function significantly affect metabolism rates, which is why dose adjustments are frequently required for patients with kidney or liver disease.

**Excretion** is the elimination of drugs and their metabolites from the body, primarily through the kidneys in urine and, for some drugs, through the liver in bile. Renal function — measured by creatinine clearance or the estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) — is the most important determinant of drug excretion for renally cleared medications. The implications for nursing practice are significant: virtually every patient over 65, most patients with diabetes or hypertension, and all patients with known chronic kidney disease have some degree of renal function impairment that affects drug clearance and requires dose adjustment or extended dosing intervals.

## The Rights of Medication Administration

The traditional five rights of medication administration — right patient, right drug, right dose, right route, and right time — have been expanded by many organizations to include additional rights including right documentation, right reason, right response, and right to refuse. The expansion reflects recognition that the original five rights, while necessary, are insufficient to prevent all categories of medication error.

**Right patient** requires using two patient identifiers — never a room number — to confirm patient identity before every medication administration. The Joint Commission requires two identifiers that are not location-based; the patient's name and date of birth are the standard. This check must be performed at every administration, not just the first, because patient transfers, room changes, and workflow interruptions create conditions where the wrong patient can receive the wrong medication even when the nurse is experienced and conscientious.

**Right drug** requires comparing the label of the medication to be administered against the original medication order — not against the medication administration record, which may itself contain errors. It requires correct interpretation of abbreviations that are frequently misread, including those on the Institute for Safe Medication Practices (ISMP) List of Error-Prone Abbreviations, Symbols, and Dose Designations. It requires recognizing look-alike/sound-alike medications — pairs that are frequently confused because of similar names or similar packaging — which the ISMP also maintains and which healthcare organizations are required to address in their safety systems.

**Right dose** requires not only confirming that the dose on the medication administration record matches the order, but verifying that the dose is within a safe and therapeutic range for this patient given their weight, age, renal function, and other relevant parameters. For pediatric patients, every dose must be weight-based and verified against maximum safe dose ranges. For high-alert medications, independent double-checking by a second nurse is required by most institutional policies.

High-alert medications — those that carry a heightened risk of significant patient harm when used in error — require additional safety precautions regardless of how commonly they are administered. The ISMP's list of high-alert medications in acute care settings includes concentrated electrolytes (including IV potassium chloride, which must never be given by direct IV push), anticoagulants, insulin, opioids, and chemotherapy agents. Many medication errors involving high-alert medications are fatal; many are preventable.

## Routes of Administration and Nursing Considerations

Each route of medication administration has specific nursing preparation, technique, and patient monitoring requirements that the nurse must know and apply.

Oral medications require assessing the patient's ability to swallow safely — dysphagia is common in post-stroke patients, elderly patients, and those with altered level of consciousness, and giving oral medications to a patient who cannot swallow safely causes aspiration. Medications that cannot be crushed — including extended-release preparations, enteric-coated tablets, sublingual medications, and many others — must be identified and alternative forms ordered before administration, not discovered after the tablet has been crushed.

Intravenous medications require verification of IV access patency before administration, compatibility assessment when multiple drugs are infusing through the same line, appropriate infusion rate for the specific medication (many drugs cause adverse effects when infused too rapidly), and monitoring for local and systemic adverse effects during and after administration.

Subcutaneous and intramuscular injections require appropriate site selection and rotation, correct injection technique for the route, and monitoring for local reactions. Insulin, low-molecular-weight heparins, and many biologic medications are commonly given subcutaneously; certain vaccines and some analgesics are commonly given intramuscularly. The vastus lateralis is the preferred IM site for infants; the deltoid and ventrogluteal sites are the preferred sites for adults.

## Concept Synthesis

This module addresses **safety**, **infection**, **tissue integrity**, and **evidence-based practice** as they appear in the pharmacology and clinical practice threads of all four curriculum phases.

Safety in medication administration is a system property, not a property of individual nurse performance. The medication safety systems — barcoded medication administration, electronic prescribing with clinical decision support, pharmacist review, automated dispensing cabinets with access controls — create multiple redundant safeguards precisely because no single safeguard is reliable. The nurse who understands this system understands both their role within it and its limitations.

Infection is addressed in this module through the technique requirements for parenteral medication administration — maintaining sterility during IV preparation and administration prevents catheter-related bloodstream infection and other nosocomial infections that cause significant morbidity and mortality.
