## Learning Objectives

This module provides the structural context within which all nursing practice occurs. By completing it, you will be able to describe how the United States healthcare system is organized, financed, and regulated — including the major payer systems, the continuum of care settings, and the regulatory bodies that govern quality and safety. You will explain what health equity means and how the social determinants of health shape the patients nurses encounter. You will identify the nurse's specific roles across care settings and understand how nursing care differs between acute, ambulatory, long-term, and community contexts. You will recognize how structural features of the healthcare system create inequity and how nurses can practice in ways that actively counteract those inequities rather than simply reflecting them.

## The U.S. Healthcare System: A Deliberate Description

To call the United States healthcare system a "system" is, in some ways, generous. It is more accurately described as a collection of intersecting markets, regulatory frameworks, insurance mechanisms, and delivery organizations that have evolved over more than a century without a coherent organizing principle. Understanding this is clinically important: the patients who arrive at your care are shaped by this system before they ever encounter you, and the care you provide is constrained by it in ways you must understand to advocate effectively.

Healthcare in the United States is delivered across a continuum that stretches from wellness promotion and primary prevention in the community to highly specialized tertiary care in academic medical centers, and from birth through the end of life. The acute care hospital is the setting most nurses encounter first in clinical training, but it represents a small portion of the actual healthcare footprint. More healthcare encounters in the United States occur in ambulatory settings — primary care offices, urgent care centers, outpatient surgical facilities, dialysis centers — than in inpatient settings. Long-term care, home health, rehabilitation, and hospice together represent another enormous sector where nurses practice. Community and public health nursing addresses entire populations rather than individual patients.

The system is financed through a patchwork of mechanisms. Medicare is the federal program that covers Americans aged 65 and older and those with qualifying disabilities; it is administered by the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) and is funded through a combination of payroll taxes, premiums, and general revenue. Medicaid is a joint federal-state program that covers Americans who meet income and categorical eligibility requirements; because each state administers its Medicaid program within federal guidelines, coverage and eligibility vary substantially across states. Employer-sponsored private insurance covers a substantial portion of working-age Americans and their dependents, with coverage quality and cost-sharing that varies enormously by employer and plan. The Affordable Care Act expanded coverage through Medicaid expansion and subsidized marketplace plans for individuals who do not have access to employer-sponsored coverage. Approximately 25 million Americans remain uninsured as of 2024 — a number that has real clinical consequences, because uninsured patients often delay care until conditions are severe, receive less preventive care, and face financial catastrophe when they do require hospitalization.

## The Nurse's Position in the Healthcare System

Nursing is the largest single profession in the U.S. healthcare system by headcount, with more than four million licensed registered nurses and more than two million licensed practical nurses. This numerical weight translates into a form of structural power that the profession has historically underutilized relative to its potential influence. Understanding your position in this system — what you are empowered to do, what institutions you operate within, and what forces shape the resources you have available — is the foundation of effective nursing advocacy.

Nurses function within healthcare organizations that are themselves positioned within the broader regulatory and market environment. The Joint Commission (TJC) accredits hospitals and other healthcare organizations, setting standards that affect everything from documentation requirements to nurse-patient staffing ratios in some states. CMS sets conditions of participation that hospitals must meet to receive Medicare and Medicaid reimbursement — giving those conditions enormous practical force regardless of their technical status as conditions rather than laws. State departments of health license hospitals, nursing homes, and home health agencies. Together these regulatory layers create the environment in which nursing practice occurs.

The interprofessional team — physicians, nurses, pharmacists, physical and occupational therapists, social workers, case managers, respiratory therapists, and others — is the vehicle through which complex care is delivered. Nursing's relationship to this team has evolved significantly over the past four decades. The autonomous professional model that emerged in the 1980s and 1990s — in which nurses increasingly understood themselves as independent practitioners with their own body of knowledge and ethical accountability — replaced the older subordinate model in which nurses were understood as physicians' assistants. Today, nursing practice operates within a framework of interprofessional collaboration in which each profession contributes distinct expertise, and no single profession can provide optimal care in isolation.

This is care coordination as an ethical obligation, not an administrative task.

## Social Determinants of Health and Health Equity

The social determinants of health are the conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age — and they account for somewhere between 30% and 55% of health outcomes, dwarfing the impact of clinical care in shaping population health. Income, education, housing, neighborhood safety, access to nutritious food, exposure to environmental toxins, social connection, and the experience of discrimination all affect health in ways that cascade across generations and across every body system.

Health equity — the principle that every person should have a fair and just opportunity to be as healthy as possible — is not the same as health equality (giving everyone the same thing). Health equity requires providing more support to those who face greater barriers, explicitly targeting inequities rather than merely avoiding explicit discrimination. A nurse who treats every patient "the same" — providing identical care regardless of the structural barriers some patients face — is not practicing equitable care. They are practicing equality in a system that was not built equally, which means the equal treatment reinforces rather than counteracts the inequity.

The NCLEX-NGN test plan's increasing emphasis on health equity, social determinants, and the whole-person assessment reflects the profession's recognition that clinical care divorced from social context is incomplete care. The competency being tested is not the ability to name the determinants — it is the ability to assess them in a clinical encounter, understand their implications for the patient's health, and incorporate that understanding into the plan of care.

## Quality and Safety Infrastructure

The modern patient safety movement emerged in 2000 when the Institute of Medicine published *To Err is Human*, which documented that between 44,000 and 98,000 Americans were dying each year as a result of preventable medical errors in hospitals. The report transformed how the healthcare system understood error — not as the product of individual negligence, but as the predictable output of poorly designed systems that created conditions in which good people made preventable mistakes.

The system-level response to this report created much of the quality and safety infrastructure that nurses work within today. The Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ) funds patient safety research and disseminates evidence-based practices. The Institute for Healthcare Improvement (IHI) has developed and spread safety tools including checklists, bundles, and simulation training. The QSEN (Quality and Safety Education for Nurses) initiative, begun in 2005, defined the six competencies that nursing education must develop: patient-centered care, teamwork and collaboration, evidence-based practice, quality improvement, safety, and informatics. These six competencies now appear in AACN Essential domains and are tested directly on the NCLEX.

For nursing practice, understanding quality and safety infrastructure means more than knowing what these organizations do. It means recognizing that the documentation requirements, the time-out procedures before procedures, the two-identifier patient identification, the fall risk assessment tools, the pressure injury prevention protocols — all of these are safety system components, not bureaucratic burdens. When a nurse skips a safety check because they are certain they know the patient, they are not making a clinical judgment — they are removing themselves from the error-prevention architecture that protects their patients and themselves.

## Concept Synthesis

This module deepens your understanding of **health promotion**, **care coordination**, **collaboration**, **leadership**, and **safety** as they appear throughout the BSN curriculum.

Health promotion in this context means more than patient education about lifestyle modification. It means understanding the structural barriers to health and actively working to address them through referral, advocacy, and care coordination. The nurse who understands the social determinants of health is a more effective promoter of health than one who operates as if all patients have equal access to the resources that health promotion requires.

Care coordination is the nurse's function of ensuring that care is continuous, comprehensive, and connected across settings, providers, and time. For Marcus Webb, care coordination means connecting him to pharmaceutical assistance, federally qualified health centers, and community resources — not just managing his glucose during a hospital stay.

Collaboration in the healthcare system means operating effectively within the interprofessional team, understanding each profession's unique contribution, and advocating for the patient's needs across professional boundaries. It also means collaborating with patients and families as partners in care, not merely as recipients of care.

Leadership and safety are inseparable in the healthcare system context. The nurse's advocacy role — speaking up when system factors are creating unsafe conditions, using quality improvement frameworks to identify and address patterns of near-misses and adverse events — is a leadership responsibility at every level of practice.
