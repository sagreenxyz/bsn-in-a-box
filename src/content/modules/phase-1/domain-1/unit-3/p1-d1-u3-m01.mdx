## Learning Objectives

This module establishes the legal framework within which nursing practice occurs — not as a set of rules to avoid violating, but as a structure that defines your professional accountability and protects both patients and practitioners. By completing this module, you will be able to define negligence and malpractice, articulate the four elements that must be proven for malpractice liability to attach, and explain the difference between a medication error and a compensable nursing negligence claim. You will describe what informed consent requires, who is legally empowered to give it, and what the nurse's specific role is in the consent process — including what to do when a patient does not truly understand what they are consenting to. You will explain HIPAA's requirements in practical clinical terms and identify the specific nursing behaviors that most commonly constitute HIPAA violations. You will describe advance directives, the Patient Self-Determination Act, and the nurse's responsibilities when a patient's stated current wishes conflict with a previously completed directive. You will identify mandatory reporting obligations and understand the legal and ethical framework for reporting suspected child, elder, or intimate partner abuse.

## The Foundation: Duty, Breach, Causation, and Harm

Nursing malpractice is professional negligence — a type of civil liability that arises when a nurse's failure to meet the standard of care causes harm to a patient. Understanding the legal framework requires understanding its four elements, because if any one of them is absent, liability does not attach. A patient can be harmed without a nurse being liable; a nurse can make a mistake without being liable; and a nurse can behave in ways that fall below the standard of care without being liable if no harm results from it. The four elements that must all be proven are duty, breach of duty, causation, and damages.

**Duty** is the easiest element to establish in a nursing malpractice case: when a registered nurse accepts responsibility for a patient's care — which occurs when the assignment is made, when the nurse receives a report, when the nurse performs any assessment or intervention — a legal duty of care exists. The nurse-patient relationship is the source of the duty, and courts almost never dispute that the duty existed.

**Breach of duty** is where most malpractice cases are actually fought. Breach occurs when the nurse's conduct falls below the standard of care — the level of care that a reasonably competent registered nurse with similar training and experience would have provided in the same or similar circumstances. The standard of care is not perfection. It is not the care provided by the most skilled nurse in the country. It is what a reasonable, competent registered nurse would do. Expert witnesses — other registered nurses — typically testify about what the standard of care required in the specific clinical situation, and whether the defendant nurse's conduct met that standard.

**Causation** requires proving that the breach of duty actually caused the harm — not just that harm occurred in the same period as the breach. This is more complex than it sounds. If a patient develops pneumonia after a nurse fails to perform ordered incentive spirometry, causation requires proving that the pneumonia was caused by the failure to perform the spirometry, not by the patient's underlying condition. The legal standard is that the breach was a "but for" cause of the harm — but for the nurse's failure to act, the harm would not have occurred.

**Damages** are the actual harm suffered — physical, emotional, financial. No matter how egregious the conduct, a malpractice claim cannot succeed if no measurable harm resulted. This is why documentation of near-misses and errors without harm, while ethically and institutionally required, generally does not result in malpractice liability, though it may result in disciplinary action.

## Informed Consent: The Patient's Right to Decide

Informed consent is not a piece of paper. It is a process — a substantive conversation between a patient and a clinician in which the patient receives adequate information to make a meaningful decision about their care. The written consent form is evidence that the conversation occurred, but signing the form does not create informed consent, and the absence of a signed form does not necessarily mean consent was absent.

What informed consent legally requires varies somewhat by jurisdiction, but the core elements are consistent. The patient must be given information about the nature of the proposed treatment or procedure, the purpose and expected benefits, the material risks, the alternatives (including the alternative of no treatment), and the prognosis if treatment is declined. "Material" risks are generally defined as risks that a reasonable patient would consider important in making the decision — a risk does not need to be probable to be material if a reasonable patient would want to know about it.

Informed consent requires that the patient be competent to consent — that is, that they have the cognitive capacity to understand the information, appreciate its implications for their situation, reason about options, and communicate a choice. Competence is presumed in adults. A patient who makes a decision the clinical team disagrees with is not necessarily incompetent — competence is a capacity assessment, not an agreement assessment. A patient who refuses a blood transfusion for religious reasons may be fully competent and exercising genuine autonomy. A patient with acute delirium who cannot consistently name themselves or identify where they are may lack the capacity to consent, requiring surrogate decision-making.

The nurse's role in the informed consent process is specific and important to understand precisely because it is often misunderstood. The nurse does not obtain informed consent — that is the responsibility of the person performing the procedure, typically the physician. The nurse's responsibilities are to ensure that the patient has had the opportunity to speak with the physician, that the patient's questions have been answered, to witness the patient's signature on the consent form, and critically, to advocate for the patient when the consent process appears inadequate — when the patient tells the nurse they are confused about what they agreed to, when a language barrier was not addressed, or when the nurse observes that the patient's questions were dismissed.

Ruth herself, during a lucid interval this morning, told you clearly and calmly: "I am a doctor. I have seen what ventilators do. I don't want that for myself."

A healthcare proxy has authority to make decisions when the patient lacks decision-making capacity — not when it is inconvenient that the patient has a preference. Ruth's lucid statement this morning may or may not constitute legally sufficient evidence of current capacity, and that determination belongs to the clinical team, ideally in consultation with ethics support. What the nurse knows, and is professionally and legally obligated to document and report, is that Ruth expressed a preference that conflicts with the proxy's directive. The nurse's documentation of this conversation, verbatim if possible, is a legally and ethically significant act that protects Ruth's right to self-determination and creates an accurate record for any subsequent legal or ethical review.

## HIPAA in Clinical Practice

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996 (HIPAA) created a federal framework for protecting the privacy and security of health information. The Privacy Rule governs the use and disclosure of Protected Health Information (PHI) — any individually identifiable health information held or transmitted by a covered entity or its business associates. Understanding HIPAA in practical clinical terms means understanding what it actually prohibits, because the most common HIPAA violations in nursing are not the result of malicious disclosure but of careless habits that are extremely easy to develop and extremely difficult to reverse once established.

PHI includes not just diagnostic information but virtually anything that could be used to identify a patient — name, address, date of birth, phone number, Social Security number, dates of service, and even geographic information smaller than a state. Disclosure of PHI without the patient's authorization is prohibited with specific exceptions: treatment, payment, and healthcare operations; public health activities; law enforcement (with conditions); and a few other narrow categories.

The most common HIPAA violation patterns in nursing include discussing patient information in shared spaces where others can hear (elevators, nurses' stations with open areas, cafeterias), accessing medical records for patients who are not under the nurse's care (including looking up the records of a celebrity patient, a neighbor, or a coworker out of curiosity), sharing patient information with family members without confirming the patient's consent, leaving computer screens with patient information visible in public areas, and disposing of documents containing PHI in regular trash rather than secure disposal containers.

Social media has added an entirely new category of HIPAA risk. Posting about patients — even without using their name, if the details are sufficiently specific to be identifying — is a HIPAA violation and has ended nursing careers. Photographs of patients, patient care areas with visible patient information, or even seemingly innocuous descriptions of interesting cases can constitute violations if they contain identifying information. The appropriate response to a clinically interesting or emotionally significant patient encounter is to discuss it in supervision, peer support, or clinical debriefing — not to post it.

## Advance Directives and the Patient Self-Determination Act

The Patient Self-Determination Act (PSDA) of 1990 requires all healthcare organizations that receive Medicare or Medicaid funding to inform patients at the time of admission about their right to complete an advance directive, to ask whether the patient has an existing advance directive, and to document the response. This requirement exists because research consistently shows that patients who have documented their end-of-life preferences receive care that more closely aligns with those preferences — a meaningful quality-of-care outcome.

Advance directives take several forms. A living will specifies the types of interventions the patient does or does not want in specific clinical circumstances — often "if I am in a terminal condition and cannot make my own decisions." A healthcare proxy designation (called a durable power of attorney for healthcare in many states) names a specific person to make healthcare decisions when the patient lacks capacity. A POLST (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment), also called MOLST in some states, is a medical order — not just a preference statement — that travels with the patient across care settings and has immediate clinical force.

The nurse's responsibilities regarding advance directives include asking about them on admission as required by PSDA, ensuring that existing directives are in the medical record and visible to the care team, communicating the patient's documented preferences to the team when relevant clinical situations arise, and — critically — advocating for those preferences when team behavior does not align with them.

## Mandatory Reporting Obligations

Nurses are mandatory reporters in every U.S. state — they are legally required to report suspected abuse of children, elders, and in many states, vulnerable adults, to the appropriate state agency. "Suspected" is the operative word: mandatory reporting does not require proof. It requires reasonable suspicion based on the nurse's professional assessment. The consequences of failing to report are significant: in most states, failure to report suspected abuse is a misdemeanor, and in some it is a felony. The nurse's professional license is also at risk.

The specific indicators of abuse that nurses must recognize differ by population. Child abuse indicators include injuries inconsistent with the stated mechanism, patterned injuries (burns, bruising in the shape of objects), delays in seeking care, behavioral indicators in the child (fear of specific adults, hypervigilance, regression), and caregiver behaviors that raise concern (inconsistent histories, deflection, irritation at questions). Elder abuse indicators include unexplained injuries, signs of neglect (poor hygiene, malnutrition, untreated conditions), financial exploitation, caregiver controlling behavior, and the patient's own disclosure.

When a nurse makes a mandatory report, the report goes to child protective services, adult protective services, or law enforcement depending on the population and jurisdiction. The nurse does not conduct an investigation — that is the job of the designated agency. The nurse's job is to recognize and report. Confidentiality — including HIPAA — does not prevent mandatory reporting; mandatory reporting obligations are an explicit exception to the privacy protections that would otherwise apply.

## Concept Synthesis

This module addresses the legal dimensions of **ethics**, **safety**, **communication**, and **leadership** as they appear throughout all four phases of this curriculum.

Ethics and law are not the same, but they are deeply intertwined in nursing practice. The legal requirements around informed consent are rooted in the ethical principle of autonomy. The mandatory reporting obligations exist because the ethical obligation to protect vulnerable people has been formalized into law. Understanding the legal framework deepens the ethical reasoning you developed in the previous module.

Safety — specifically, patient safety from harm caused by the healthcare system itself — is the ultimate purpose of the legal framework you studied in this module. Malpractice liability is designed not to punish individual nurses but to create accountability structures that incentivize safe practice. HIPAA protections are safety measures for patient privacy and dignity. Mandatory reporting requirements are safety measures for vulnerable populations.

Communication appears in this module as both a legal obligation and a legal risk. The consent process is a communication process. Documentation of clinical events, patient statements, and team communications is a legal communication that creates the record of care. HIPAA governs who may receive clinical communications and under what conditions.

Leadership as a legal concept means understanding that silence in the face of unsafe or unethical practice is not neutrality — it is a choice with legal and professional consequences. The nurse who observes conduct that falls below the standard of care and says nothing has not protected themselves. They have potentially become a party to the harm that results.
