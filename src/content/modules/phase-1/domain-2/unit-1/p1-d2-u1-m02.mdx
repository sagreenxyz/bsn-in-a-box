## Learning Objectives

Vital signs — temperature, pulse, respiration, blood pressure, and oxygen saturation — are the most frequently performed nursing assessment and among the most consequential. By completing this module, you will be able to measure each vital sign using correct technique and explain why technique matters clinically, interpret vital sign values in the context of the patient's age, baseline, diagnosis, and current clinical situation, recognize the clinical significance of abnormal vital sign patterns and trends over time, describe the physiological mechanisms that underlie each vital sign parameter, and initiate an appropriate nursing response to critical vital sign values including the correct communication to the interprofessional team using SBAR format.

## Temperature: Regulation, Measurement, and Clinical Significance

Body temperature is the product of the balance between heat production and heat loss, regulated by the hypothalamus with remarkable precision in healthy adults. Core body temperature is normally maintained between 36.5°C and 37.5°C (97.7°F to 99.5°F), with diurnal variation of approximately 1°C — lower in the early morning and highest in the late afternoon and early evening. This normal variation means that a temperature of 37.8°C (100°F) at 8 AM carries different significance than the same value at 4 PM.

Fever — a temperature above 38°C (100.4°F) — is not an error in thermoregulation but a regulated upward shift of the hypothalamic set point in response to pyrogens, most commonly infectious in origin. Understanding this mechanism has clinical implications: fever is itself a physiological response that serves functions including inhibiting bacterial replication and enhancing immune function. The clinical decision of whether to treat fever is not simply "bring the number down" — it is a nuanced judgment about the patient's overall status, the underlying cause, and whether the fever is causing sufficient physiological burden to warrant treatment.

Hypothermia — a core temperature below 35°C (95°F) — is a medical emergency that impairs cardiac conduction, respiratory function, coagulation, and neurological function in a progressively severe cascade. Its causes range from environmental exposure to septic shock to severe endocrine dysfunction. Measurement accuracy becomes critical when hypothermia is suspected — tympanic or axillary thermometers are unreliable at extreme temperatures, and rectal or esophageal core temperature measurement is required.

## Pulse: Rate, Rhythm, and Character

The pulse is the arterial pressure wave generated by left ventricular contraction and transmitted through the arterial tree. Pulse rate, rhythm, and character all carry diagnostic information. A normal adult resting pulse is 60 to 100 beats per minute, with the lower end of normal reflecting effective cardiovascular conditioning and the upper end requiring assessment to distinguish sinus tachycardia from arrhythmia.

Pulse assessment includes more than rate. Rhythm — whether the beats arrive at regular or irregular intervals — is clinically significant: an irregularly irregular rhythm suggests atrial fibrillation, a common arrhythmia with substantial stroke risk. Character — the amplitude and quality of the pulse wave — reflects stroke volume and vascular compliance. A bounding pulse with high amplitude suggests high stroke volume or widened pulse pressure (as in aortic regurgitation). A thready pulse with low amplitude suggests reduced stroke volume, most commonly from hypovolemia or pump failure. Pulsus alternans — alternating strong and weak beats — is a sign of significant left ventricular dysfunction.

## Blood Pressure: Interpretation Beyond the Number

Blood pressure is the product of cardiac output and systemic vascular resistance, and understanding this relationship is prerequisite to interpreting blood pressure values accurately. The same blood pressure number can reflect completely different physiological states: a blood pressure of 90/60 in a pregnant woman at 20 weeks gestation is often normal; the same number in an elderly patient with known hypertension who was 160/90 yesterday is a hemodynamic emergency.

Hypertension — defined by the American College of Cardiology and American Heart Association as sustained blood pressure at or above 130/80 mmHg — affects nearly half of U.S. adults and is the most important modifiable risk factor for cardiovascular disease. The nursing assessment of blood pressure requires scrupulous technique: proper cuff size for the patient's arm circumference (a common source of measurement error), measurement in a seated position after five minutes of rest, both arms on first assessment, avoidance of tight clothing over the arm, and documentation of position, site, and cuff size.

## Oxygen Saturation and Respiratory Rate

Pulse oximetry measures oxygen saturation (SpO2) non-invasively via light absorption by hemoglobin, providing a continuous real-time surrogate for arterial oxygen saturation (SaO2). Normal SpO2 is 95-100% in healthy adults. An SpO2 below 90% represents significant hypoxemia requiring immediate assessment and intervention. Critical limitations of pulse oximetry include inaccuracy in the presence of carbon monoxide poisoning (which is read as oxyhemoglobin, producing falsely normal readings), inaccuracy in severe peripheral vasoconstriction or anemia, and inability to detect hypercarbia — elevated carbon dioxide — which can be present with normal oxygen saturation in patients receiving supplemental oxygen.

Respiratory rate is the most commonly poorly measured vital sign in clinical practice, often estimated rather than counted for a full 60 seconds. An accurate respiratory rate is one of the most sensitive early indicators of clinical deterioration — subtle tachypnea at 20-22 breaths per minute in a patient who was previously at 14-16 is often the first detectable sign of sepsis, pulmonary embolism, or impending respiratory failure. The habit of actually counting respirations for a full minute, every time, is a patient safety behavior that experienced nurses often abandon and that beginners should establish and never abandon.
