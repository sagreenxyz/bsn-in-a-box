## Learning Objectives

Health assessment is the foundation of every nursing action you will take for the rest of your career. By completing this module, you will understand what a comprehensive health assessment includes and why each component exists — not as a checklist to complete but as a purposeful, theoretically grounded clinical inquiry. You will be able to conduct a health history interview using therapeutic communication techniques, explain the relationship between subjective data (what the patient tells you) and objective data (what you observe and measure), describe the four assessment techniques — inspection, palpation, percussion, and auscultation — and when each is appropriate. You will recognize the developmental and cultural factors that modify assessment findings across the lifespan and across populations, and you will understand how nursing assessment differs from and complements medical assessment in its goals and its scope.

<ThresholdConcept title="Assessment Is Interpretation, Not Data Collection">
Students beginning clinical education often understand health assessment as a data collection exercise: ask the questions on the form, record the numbers, move on. This understanding is incomplete in a way that has direct patient safety implications. A nurse who understands assessment as data collection records a blood pressure of 158/96 and moves to the next patient. A nurse who understands assessment as interpretation asks: What is this patient's baseline blood pressure? What was it yesterday? What medications is she on, and is she taking them? What are her symptoms? What does this value mean in the context of her kidney disease? What does it mean for the nursing plan of care?

Assessment is the nursing process's first and most clinically consequential step because the quality of every subsequent step — diagnosis, planning, implementation, and evaluation — depends on the quality of the initial assessment. An incomplete assessment produces an incomplete clinical picture. An inaccurate assessment produces an inaccurate clinical picture. In both cases, the nursing care built on that foundation is compromised before it begins.

Crossing this threshold means understanding that you are not filling in boxes when you assess a patient — you are constructing a clinical understanding of a specific human being at a specific moment in their health trajectory. Every finding means something, and your job is to understand what.
</ThresholdConcept>

## The Health History Interview

The health history is the subjective component of the health assessment — the information the patient provides about their health through a structured interview. It is not a questionnaire given to the patient to complete; it is a conversation conducted by a skilled clinician who uses verbal and nonverbal communication to elicit accurate, complete information that the patient might not volunteer spontaneously and might not know to offer unprompted.

The components of a comprehensive health history include the biographic data (name, age, gender, occupation, and other identifying information), the chief complaint or reason for seeking care stated in the patient's own words, the history of present illness developed using the OLDCART or PQRSTU frameworks to characterize every symptom fully, the past medical and surgical history, the current medications including prescription, over-the-counter, herbal, and supplement use, known allergies and the nature of allergic reactions, the family history with attention to heritable conditions, the social history including living situation, support systems, occupation, substance use, and sexuality and reproductive history, and the review of systems — a systematic survey of each body system to identify symptoms the patient has not mentioned spontaneously.

The interview technique that elicits the most complete health history is a blend of open-ended and closed-ended questioning. Open-ended questions — "Tell me more about what you've been experiencing" — create space for the patient to narrate their experience in their own words, often revealing information that a questionnaire would miss. Closed-ended questions — "Does the pain radiate to your left arm?" — efficiently verify specific details. Active listening, which includes maintaining appropriate eye contact, using minimal encouragers, summarizing and paraphrasing, and allowing comfortable silences, communicates to the patient that what they are saying matters and that you are fully present.

As you explore further, Eleanor mentions that she has been urinating much more frequently than usual, including twice during the night — a symptom she hadn't volunteered because she assumed it was a consequence of her diabetes that "everyone must have." The health history, conducted with attention and skilled interviewing technique, reveals a clinical picture of possible worsening renal function or suboptimal glycemic control — findings that have significant implications for Eleanor's management that would have been missed by a questionnaire alone.

## Objective Data: The Physical Examination

While the health history provides the subjective clinical picture, the physical examination generates the objective data that either confirms, contradicts, or adds complexity to what the patient has reported. The four techniques of physical examination — inspection, palpation, percussion, and auscultation — are applied in a specific sequence that varies by body system.

Inspection is the first technique applied in every system and is often undervalued because it appears passive. Skilled inspection requires training the eye to notice what is actually present rather than confirming what is expected. Respiratory inspection includes noting the rate, depth, and regularity of breathing, the use of accessory muscles, chest symmetry, and skin color. Abdominal inspection notes contour, symmetry, visible peristalsis, and skin characteristics before any touch.

Palpation applies touch to assess texture, temperature, moisture, tenderness, organ size, and the presence of masses. Light palpation uses approximately one centimeter of depth and is used for surface structures and tenderness assessment before deeper structures are examined. Deep palpation uses four to five centimeters of depth and is used to assess deeper organs and identify masses. Ballottement is a specific palpation technique used to detect fluid-filled structures or floating masses.

Percussion generates sounds that indicate the density of underlying tissue: resonance over air-filled lung tissue, dullness over solid or fluid-filled tissue, tympany over hollow organ structures. It is most commonly used in pulmonary and abdominal assessment and requires considerable practice to develop reliability. Auscultation uses a stethoscope to assess breath sounds, heart sounds, bowel sounds, and vascular sounds, and is applied after inspection, palpation, and percussion in every system except the abdomen — where auscultation precedes palpation to avoid artificially altering bowel sounds.

## Developmental Considerations in Assessment

Health assessment findings are meaningless without a developmental context. A respiratory rate of 40 breaths per minute is a crisis finding in an adult and a normal finding in a newborn. A blood pressure of 100/60 is within normal range for a young adult woman and should be urgently investigated in a 70-year-old man who was previously hypertensive. Cognitive assessment findings that suggest pathology in a 40-year-old may represent normal aging changes in an 85-year-old, while cognitive changes that are attributed to normal aging in an older adult may in fact reflect early dementia that warrants evaluation.

The developmental framework that nurses use spans the entire lifespan — from neonatal assessment through the comprehensive geriatric assessment of frail older adults. Across this span, anatomic and physiologic norms shift substantially, and the nurse who applies adult norms to pediatric patients or geriatric patients without adjustment is not performing a health assessment — they are performing an assessment mismatch that will produce inaccurate conclusions.

## Cultural Considerations in Assessment

Culture shapes health behaviors, symptom expression, the meaning patients attach to illness, and the help-seeking behaviors that determine when and how patients access care. A nurse who conducts health assessments without cultural awareness will miss significant clinical information and will damage the therapeutic relationship that makes accurate assessment possible.

Cultural competence in assessment does not mean knowing a fixed set of facts about specific cultural groups — that approach leads to stereotyping and misses the enormous diversity within any cultural category. It means approaching every patient with genuine curiosity about what their health experience means to them, asking about their explanatory model of illness ("What do you think is causing your symptoms? What concerns you most about it?"), and incorporating cultural and spiritual considerations into the plan of care in ways that respect the patient's values without compromising safety.
